The Rejection Ratio
Joe Clark ~~ Aug 1, 2003

I have often noticed in my life that I am naturally drawn to the people who have time for me. On the flip side, the people who don't have any time to spare annoy me. There are a lot of people in this world that are so crazy busy that their schedules virtually block me out (that could always be by design, and that is taken into consideration here as well). I would like to present some mathematical ideas for a way to characterize the actions of people and for rating people as to how "rejective" they are. I know, I know, such characterization of people is not very nice, but oh well. Rejection isn't very nice either.

First of all, I would like to make the rejection ratio a fairly open concept. I want to present some guidelines here and ideas for some possible values, but in the end the actual values assigned depend on the person and the situation at hand. Just try to be consistent in your own use of this, and the ratio should be meaningful to determine who is rejective and who is not. The values I assign to the various types of rejection are characteristic of what I generally consider to be the hurtfulness of the various kinds of rejection..you'll see what I mean.

Okay, now let's move on to discussing the preliminaries. Rejection is defined as when you invite someone to do something with you, and they decline your offer for any reason. Now, the "rejectional value" of the refusal may approach 0, depending on how good of an excuse they have, or it may be negative, if they turn the rejection into a counter-invitation or if they do not reject you at all. Keep those things in mind. The most common types of invitations that this ratio will deal with are the following: in person conversations, phone calls, IM conversations, emails, group outings, and one-on-one outings. Let's analyze each in a little more detail...

In-person conversations: These conversations are the conversations that come up fairly randomly -- at church, at work, in a chance meeting at Walmart. I guess I'm mostly thinking here of the truly Walmartish occasions, not the greeting time at church or the random work conversations. Here are some guidelines:

  • If the other person sees you but ignores you, assign 5 rejection points (RPs).
  • If the other person sees you and begins talking to you, but then someone else comes along and they begin talking solely to the other person, assign 4 RPs.
  • If the other person says something like, "Hi I'd love to stay and talk but I have to go wash my cat," assign 5 RPs.
  • If the other person talks for a few moments, then leaves, assign 4 RPs.
  • If the other person talks until you feel the conversation is rightfully over, assign 0 RPs.
  • If the other person talks longer than you think is necessary, assign -3 RPs (since they are giving extra time to talk to you).

Phone calls: Phone calls are interesting phenomena, and their importance depends to a large extent on the personal preferences of the people involved. Some people talk on the phone for hours, some people get off as quick as possible....here are my guidelines.

  • If you call someone for the first time in a long time, and they say they are busy, and don't offer to talk sometime later, assign 10 RPs.
  • If you call someone for the first time in a long time, and they say they are busy, but do offer to call back, and never do, assign 15 RPs.
  • If you call someone for the first time in a long time, and they say they are busy, but do offer to call back, and they do, assign points as for a normal conversation below.
  • If you call someone for the first time in a long time, and they speak to you on the phone, but they seem to be bored talking to you and giving you the vibe, "Hang up, you boring person", assign 5 RPs.
  • If you call someone for the first time in a long time, and they speak to you on the phone, and they seem happy to talk to you and the conversation goes well, assign 0 RPs.
  • If you call someone for the second time within a week, assign points at half the value given above. If the conversation goes well, assign -3 RPs, as they are showing a little extra interest in you by talking for the 2nd time in the week.
  • If you call someone for more than the second time in a week, assign points at 1/4 the value given to begin with. If the conversation goes well, assign -5 RPs..they must really think you're cool to keep talking to you.

IM conversations: IM conversations are those fun online conversations. They are fairly informal, and can last for a few hours or a few seconds.

  • If you IM someone for the first time in more than 3 days, and they ignore the request, assign 5 RPs (but be prepared to rescind the penalty if you find out they had a good excuse for not responding).
  • If you IM someone for the first time in more than 3 days, and they say something like, "Hi, I'm shockingly busy, much much too busy for you," assign 10 RPs.
  • If you IM someone for the first time in more than 3 days, and they say they're too busy, but offer to talk later, assign 0 RPs. If 2 days pass without a word via email or other means, add 7 RPs.
  • If you IM someone for the first time in more than 3 days, and the conversation is okay, but ends without a real ending, assign 1 RP (this ending would seem to say they were bored talking with you). However, if they were the last person to post a message, assign -1 RPs (since you were the one looking bored and unresponsive).
  • If you IM someone for the first time in 1-3 days, use the scale above, but cut the RPs in half.
  • If you IM someone more than once a day, no RPs should be assigned. You can't ask for too much ya know.

Emails: Email rejection can vary a lot. Some people you hear from once a month or once a year, and that's all right (kinda like the Christmas letter). Some people you expect to hear from at least once a week...these factors have to be taken into consideration in the guidelines below.

  • If you email someone expecting a response (having at least one clear question in the email), and they fail to respond within a month, assign 10 RPs. For each additional month until you don't care anymore, send a reminder/refresher email, and add 1 to the number of RPs assigned (11, then 12, etc) if they don't respond.
  • If you email someone expecting a response (having at least one clear question in the email), and they respond within a month but not within a week, assign 3 RPs.
  • If you email someone expecting a response (having at least one clear question in the email), and they respond within a week but longer than 3 days, assign 1 RP.
  • If you email someone expecting a response (having at least one clear question in the email), and they respond in 1-3 days, assign 0 RPs.
  • If you email someone expecting a response (having at least one clear question in the email), and they respond within 24 hours, assign -3 RPs. Wow.
  • If the email response above contains significantly more personal content than your email did, add -3 RPs.
  • If the email response above contains significantly less personal content than your email did, add 3 RPs.

Group outings: These outings could be church group social events, coworker parties, movies, or anything else where a group is involved. Care must be taken not to assign RPs where there is a valid reason for not attending the event (eg a schedule conflict).

  • If the group size is larger than 7, do not assign RPs either way, unless the invited person is an outsider to the group or has some other valid reason for not attending the event...if the person does attend the event in that case, assign -5 RPs -- that person is showing an interest in joining you in the event even if it's something new for them or possibly awkward.
  • If the event requires money, weigh the cost and the person's ability to pay honestly.
  • If the event requires a substantial amount of time, weigh that consideration honestly.
  • If there is no obvious reason for the refusal, assign 1 RP....group events are typically casual events, and a person needs to feel free to come or go as they please. But, because you are inviting, it is some amount of rejection for them to say no. To say no once is all right (hence 1 RP -- a small value), but to say no multiple times adds up.

One-on-one outings: Ahh, we come to the most awkward, most RP-sensitive invitations.

  • If the person refuses your invitation without giving a reason, assign 15 RPs. (If they don't even bother to tell you why, then they deserve it.)
  • If the person refuses your invitation, but offers a reasonable alternative, and sticks to it and the alternative event happens, assign -5 RPs -- they are showing an interest by offering an alternative.
  • If the person accepts your invitation at first, but then declines without a good reason within an "ok" amount of time before the event, assign 2 RPs. Changing one's mind is annoying, but since they gave you ample notice, it's not a huge deal.
  • If the person accepts your invitation at first, but then declines without a good reason at the last minute, assign 10 RPs.
  • If the person accepts your invitation at first, but doesn't show up, assign 15 RPs.
  • If the person accepts your invitation, and the event goes well, and they invite you to something in return, assign -15 RPs.
  • If the person accepts your invitation, but they seem bored, distracted, or annoyed during the event, assign 4 RPs.
  • If the person accepts your invitation, and the event goes well, but conversation is limited to surface issues, add 3 RPs. On the other hand, if conversation is "deep" in your eyes, add -3 RPs.

The past experience option: If you so choose, you may want to weight your RP assignments based on past experience. This can go either way. If someone has a low rejection ratio (RR) in your life, meaning they do not normally reject you, and they reject you this time, perhaps they should be given a break. On the other hand, if a pattern develops, the RR possibly should be inflated moreso, because one who goes from good friend to continual rejecter probably should have a high RR. Now, if someone has a high RR in your life, you may think twice before lowering it by a significant amount (maybe it was a fluke). However, depending on the situation, maybe they have had a change of heart, in which case the RR should be deflated moreso, since the person is taking a new interest in you for some reason.

There you have it....my initial guidelines for a "rejection ratio" index that you can assign to all your friends and enemies (and they can assign one to you as well). Now you can have a number to gauge just how rejective the people around you are (or aren't), and maybe that number can give you ammunition for saying things like, "You're always too busy for me, you cat washer you." Enjoy, and don't be too evil with your new-found categorization abilities.
