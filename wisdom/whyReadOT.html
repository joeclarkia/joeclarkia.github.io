Question #44

Joe Clark
Dec 11, 2001

Is there any value in reading the Old Testament?

In the realms of popular Christianity, at times the comment is heard which goes something like this, "Why read the Old Testament? The stories are fables, the wars are gruesome, the rules are harsh, and the ideas are stale with the passing of time." Is this true? What can be said about this? I believe that there is indeed value in the Old Testament, for several reasons. Let me share just a few with you.

  1. The Old Testament chronicles the beginnings of all things and gives insight into ultimate purpose. "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth." Those few words, and the others in the book of Genesis, tell the story of the start of all things: the universe, the earth, the natural world, humanity, and God's interactions with the world. Paradigms are set. God created. God chose to relate to mankind. God gave freewill. God chose to still pursue mankind, even after the Fall. These profound truths affect us every day. They are foundational to much of our collective thinking for the past 6000 years. Truly understanding where we come from is important to where we are and where we are heading, and we cannot see the past clearly without looking to the Old Testament.
  2. The Old Testament gives historical perspective. It has often been said that those who forget the past are doomed to repeat it. Few doubt the importance of history. And when that history is shared by so many people (perhaps at least 1/3 of the world's population), it is all the more important. The writings of the Old Testament form the basis of belief for the Jewish, Christian, and Muslim faiths. Some of the epic stories found in the Old Testament are the creation, Abraham, Moses, Joshua, Ruth, David, the Psalms, Solomon, the corruption of the nation of Israel, the fall of Jerusalem, Daniel, Esther, and the other prophets. Sometimes these stories are "far out." Sometimes they are tragic. Sometimes they tell tales of love and devotion. They portray the full range of human experiences and emotions. Someone in almost any condition can find a passage in the Old Testament to elaborate on their joy, their sorrow, their problem, or their victory. Everyone can learn something from these documentaries, and we sell ourselves short if we ignore them.
  3. The New Testament cannot be properly understood without understanding the Old Testament. Looking through the books of the New Testament, it would be hard to find one book that does not refer back to the Old Testament. In the gospels, the Old Testament provides a cultural backdrop for the Jewish people and prophecies regarding the coming Messiah. In Acts, one can only truly understand the conflicts between the Jews and the Christians by understanding their similarities and differences that have their roots in the Old Testament. In the epistles, many times the authors make reference to the Old Testament (see especially Hebrews). The Story of the Ages -- of God's ceaseless pursuit of humankind -- is a timeless thread, and each part is important, from God walking in the morning mist in the garden of Eden to Abraham and Isaac and their foreshadowing of the cross, to the rise and fall of the nations of Israel and Judah, to the coming of God in the flesh in the New Testament.
  4. The Old Testament provides a fuller picture of the heart and nature of God. Perhaps the most important reason for esteeming the Old Testament, a fact that is wrapped up in all the previous ones, is that the Old Testament shows us who God is. We see a number of things about God from looking into the Old Testament:
    • God created humanity to reflect his glory and to enjoy living in His presence.
    • God valued free choice enough to allow mankind to choose their path (unfortunately, we chose the wrong path).
    • God did not leave humanity after the fall, but continued to favor us and work on earth as people looked to Him.
    • God provided rules for life in the Ten Commandments -- guidelines to order our life around.
    • God's heart can be saddened by those who turn away from him, much as our hearts can be saddened by unrequited love.

    These attributes of God or God's interaction with people on earth set the stage for God's work in the New Testament. Without the background information, the stories of the New Testament would still be true, but not as...complete.

With these ideas in mind, it should be clear that the Old Testament plays a vital role in our thinking as Christians. But, no honest look at the Old Testament would be complete without examining some of the reasons that people raise the question we're looking at. A couple of these were mentioned above, but here they are in more detail:

  1. "The Old Testament is a book of stories...nice, but not true." This is a frequently heard argument, and it's true that nothing in history can be proven 100% true. A measure of faith is necessary, but it is striking how certain things in the Old Testament have been declared "more probable" by recent archeological evidence, especially artifacts from the empires after the fall of Jerusalem.
  2. "The stories of the Old Testament show God to be a mean-spirited being bent on the destruction of innocent people." This is another attack on the character of God. A quick glance at such things as God's guidance to Joshua to destroy the Canaanites and the prophets declaring doom for the wayward nations could in fact provoke one to think of God in a negative way. But a more balanced view upholds God's just, if not loving, nature in the Old Testament. In Deuteronomy 5:9,10, God states "I, the LORD your God, am a jealous God, punishing the children for the sin of the fathers to the third and fourth generation of those who hate me, but showing love to a thousand [generations] of those who love me and keep my commandments." So God might be seen as a rather strict Father, but not mean-spirited. Lamentations 3:32 speaks of God like this: "Though he brings grief, he will show compassion, so great is his unfailing love." And in Ezekiel 16:8, God speaks of his love for His wayward people: "'Later I passed by, and when I looked at you and saw that you were old enough for love, I spread the corner of my garment over you....'" God created love, and God created justice, and their perfect balance is displayed throughout the Bible, although it's hard for us to see and understand at times.
  3. "The rules in the Old Testament are impossible to keep." Well, that's true. God knew that when he made them. God's purposes in setting forth the Law are often debated. It's possible that they are specific laws for a specific group of people, and some of them only make sense in the context of ancient Israel. It's possible that they are a tool to show us our need for God by helping us realize that we cannot be perfect. In any case, I believe that the rules of the Old Testament, while they shouldn't be downplayed, should be balanced by a consideration of how those laws reflect God's character, first in their original context, and also for us today.

So, after these ruminations, what can we conclude? Hopefully, we can conclude that the Old Testament is an important piece in discovering who God is and how we relates to us and how we should relate to Him. It's words are sometimes hard to understand, but I believe taking the effort to read and understand the Old Testament will prove very beneficial to our spiritual walk.
